The Capitol Gains Edge

Here’s the thing most options traders get wrong.

They find a great trade idea. They know the stock is going to move. They buy their options... and then the clock starts ticking.

Every single day, their options lose a little value just from time passing. This is called theta, and it works like a meter running on a cab. The longer you sit there waiting, the more it costs you.

So what happens? The stock hasn’t moved yet, but the meter has eaten into their profits. They panic. They close early. They were right about where the stock was going, but they ran out of patience because time itself was bleeding them dry.

That’s the problem Frank and I built Capitol Gains to solve.

Every trade we send you is designed so that theta equals zero. Here’s what I mean.

Picture a stakeout. Most traders are sitting in a cab with the meter running. Every hour they wait costs more money. Eventually they have to give up and go home because the cost of waiting is eating through their budget.

But you? You’ve parked your own car. You brought your own coffee. You can sit there all night, all week, all month. It costs you nothing to wait. And when the stock finally makes its move, you’re still right there, fully funded, ready to act.

That’s what zero theta means. We build every trade with two sides that cancel each other’s time decay out. One side pays rent, the other collects rent, and they balance to zero. You’re living rent-free while waiting for the trade to play out.

The Bottom Line

You don’t have to be right about when. You just have to be right about where. And that changes everything.

How We Structure Every Trade

Every Capitol Gains trade is built with two parts (called “legs” in options lingo) that balance each other:

Theta 1

The high-confidence side. This is where we feel strongest about the stock moving in a certain direction.

Theta 2

The bonus side. This part can make extra money if the stock makes a really big move.

We combine these two parts so that theta equals zero. Time decay doesn’t cost you anything while you wait.

Reading the Trade Alerts

When you see a Capitol Gains trade alert, it uses shorthand. Here’s what everything means:

Trade Shorthand

+C

Buy a Call (you pay to open the trade)

-C

Sell a Call (you collect money to open the trade)

+P

Buy a Put (you pay to open the trade)

-P

Sell a Put (you collect money to open the trade)

Key Terms

Premium

The price of an option. It’s what you pay to buy one, or what you collect when you sell one. Think of it like a ticket price.

Debit vs. Credit

Debit = a trade where you pay money up front (you spend now, hoping to make more later). Credit = a trade where you collect money up front (you get paid now, hoping the trade stays in your favor).

Buy-to-Open (BTO) & Sell-to-Open (STO)

Buy-to-open means you’re starting a new trade by buying an option. Sell-to-open means you’re starting a new trade by selling an option. These are the “opening” orders you’ll place with your broker.

Theta Labels on Trades

Each trade type has a theta label that tells you how time decay affects it:

Bullish Theta

Time is on your side. Each day that passes, you make a little money from decay.

Bearish Theta

Time works against you. Each day that passes, you lose a little money from decay.

Zero Theta

Balanced. Time passing doesn’t help or hurt you.

Strike Selection: Where to Place Your Bets

Every option has a “strike price,” which is the price you’re betting the stock will be above (for calls) or below (for puts). Options come in three types based on where the strike sits compared to the stock’s current price:

At-the-Money (ATM)

The strike price is right at or very close to where the stock is trading now.

Stock is at $100, you buy the $100 call.

ATM options have the most theta in dollar terms because the market is most unsure about what’ll happen next.

In-the-Money (ITM)

The option already has real value built in.

Stock is at $100, you buy the $90 call. That call is worth at least $10 right now.

ITM options have the smallest theta compared to what you paid. They lose value slower as a percentage of their price.

Out-of-the-Money (OTM)

The option has no real value yet. It’s all hope, no built-in value. Just a bet the stock will move.

Stock is at $100, you buy the $110 call. Worthless unless stock climbs above $110.

OTM options have huge theta compared to what you paid. They lose value fast.

The Decay Hierarchy

For Calls: ITM > ATM > OTM   (ITM decays slowest, OTM decays fastest as %)
For Puts: OTM > ATM > ITM   (reversed because put values work the other way)

Expiration Selection: How Much Time to Buy

Options have expiration dates. That’s the day they either become valuable or expire worthless. How far out you go matters:

Near-Term Expirations

Days to a few weeks out. Have the most theta. Time decay speeds up fast as expiration gets closer, like a candle burning faster as it gets shorter.

Far-Term Expirations

Months out. Have the least theta. The decay is gentle because there’s still plenty of time for things to happen.

Think of it like buying milk. Milk expiring tomorrow is practically worthless at the store. Milk expiring in two weeks still has most of its value. Options work the same way.

Understanding Volatility: The Two Types

Volatility is how much a stock moves around. Capitol Gains tracks two kinds:

Implied Volatility (IV)

What the options market expects to happen. High IV means options are expensive because traders are expecting big moves. Low IV means options are cheap.

Think of IV as the weather forecast. It’s what people think will happen.
Realized Volatility (RV)

What’s actually happening. How much is the stock really moving day to day?

Think of RV as the actual weather. It’s what’s happening outside your window right now.

The relationship between these two tells us which trades to use.

The Key Insight

When a stock is actually moving (high RV), own options to ride that movement. When options are expensive but the stock is quiet (high IV, low RV), use balanced spreads so you’re not paying inflated daily rent while waiting.

The Trade Types Explained

Long Calls (Bull Calls)

+C (buy a call) Bearish Theta Best when: High RV, Low IV Approval: Level 2

What it is: Simply buying a call option, which is a bet the stock will go up. If the stock rises above your strike price, the call becomes more valuable. If it doesn’t, you lose the premium (the price you paid).

You’re buying a lottery ticket, but one where the drawing happens every day and the stock’s movement determines if you win. You want cheap tickets (low IV) on a game that’s actually paying out often (high RV).
Example Trade:
Buy-to-open 1 MSFT Jan 26 $420 Call for $8.50 (no more than $9.00)

Long Puts (Bear Puts)

+P (buy a put) Bearish Theta Best when: High RV, Low IV Approval: Level 2

What it is: Buying a put option, which is a bet the stock will go down. If the stock drops below your strike price, the put becomes more valuable. If it doesn’t, you lose the premium.

Think of it like buying insurance on a stock you think is going to crash. You pay a small amount up front (the premium), and if the stock tanks, your insurance pays off. If nothing bad happens, you just lose the cost of the insurance.
Example Trade:
Buy-to-open 1 TSLA Feb 21 $350 Put for $12.00 (no more than $12.50)

Long Call Spreads (Bull Call Spreads)

+C, -C (buy a call, sell a call) Zero Theta Best when: Higher IV Approval: Level 3

What it is: Buy an ITM call, sell an OTM call at a higher strike. Both same expiration.

How it works: You’re buying the right to profit if the stock goes up, but you’re capping how much you can make by selling a call above. The short strike is your target, and that’s where your max profit happens.

You’re buying a ticket to a concert (the long call), but you’re selling the backstage pass rights (the short call) to make the ticket cheaper. You get to enjoy the show up to a point, but you’ve given up the chance for the ultimate VIP experience.
Stock at $100. Buy the $95 call, sell the $105 call. You profit as the stock rises toward $105, with max profit at $105 or above. Your cost is lower because you sold that $105 call.
Example Trade:
Buy-to-open 1 AAPL Mar 21 $220 Call for $12.00
Sell-to-open 1 AAPL Mar 21 $235 Call for $5.50
Net debit: $6.50 (no more than $7.00)

Short Call Spreads (Bear Call Spreads)

-C, +C (sell a call, buy a call) Bullish Theta Best when: Higher IV, stock flat or down Approval: Level 3

What it is: Sell an ITM call, buy an OTM call at a higher strike. The reverse of above.

How it works: You collect money up front by selling a call, and buy a cheaper one above as protection. The short strike is your target. You want the stock at or below that level when the options expire.

You’re the casino now. You’re betting the stock won’t go up much, collecting money from someone who thinks it will. The long call is your insurance policy that caps your losses if you’re wrong.
Example Trade:
Sell-to-open 1 NVDA Feb 21 $140 Call for $8.00
Buy-to-open 1 NVDA Feb 21 $150 Call for $4.00
Net credit: $4.00 (no less than $3.75)

Long Put Spreads (Bear Put Spreads)

+P, -P (buy a put, sell a put) Bearish Theta Best when: Higher IV, stock falling Approval: Level 3

What it is: Buy an ITM put, sell an OTM put at a lower strike.

How it works: You’re betting on a decline but capping your profit at the short strike. The short strike is your target.

You’re buying home insurance (the long put) but agreeing that you’ll only be covered down to a certain damage level (the short put). Cheaper than full coverage, but limited protection.
Example Trade:
Buy-to-open 1 META Mar 21 $580 Put for $18.00
Sell-to-open 1 META Mar 21 $560 Put for $11.00
Net debit: $7.00 (no more than $7.50)

Short Put Spreads (Bull Put Spreads)

-P, +P (sell a put, buy a put) Bullish Theta Best when: Higher IV, stock flat or rising Approval: Level 3

What it is: Sell an ITM put, buy an OTM put at a lower strike.

How it works: You collect money up front by betting the stock won’t fall much. The long put below is your safety net that limits how much you can lose.

A friend says, “I bet you $350 this stock won’t drop below $190.” If you’re right and it stays above $190, you keep the $350. If you’re wrong, you owe them, but the safety net (long put) caps how much.
Example Trade:
Sell-to-open 1 GOOGL Feb 21 $190 Put for $6.50
Buy-to-open 1 GOOGL Feb 21 $180 Put for $3.00
Net credit: $3.50 (no less than $3.25)

Cash-Secured Puts

-P (sell a put) Bullish Theta Best when: High probability, Higher IV Approval: Level 2

What it is: Selling a put on its own, without another option to go with it. You keep enough cash in your account to buy the shares if the stock drops below your strike and your broker makes you follow through (this is called “getting assigned”).

How it works: You sell a put below the current stock price, collecting the premium. If the stock stays above your strike, you keep the money. If it falls below, you’re required to buy the shares at that strike. But you wanted to own them at that price anyway.

You’re getting paid to put in a limit order. Someone pays you to agree that you’ll buy their stock if it drops to a certain price. If it never gets there, you just pocket the payment.
Example Trade:
Sell-to-open 1 AMD Jan 17 $120 Put for $3.20 (no less than $3.00)
(Keep $12,000 cash in your account to cover the purchase)

Call Calendars

+C, -C (diff. expirations) Bullish Theta Best when: Near-term options priced higher than far-term Approval: Level 3

What it is: Buy a longer-dated call, sell a shorter-dated call at the same strike.

How it works: The near-term option you sold loses value faster than the far-term option you bought. You make money from that difference. The strike is your target. You want the stock to land right there when the short option expires.

You’re renting out a room in your house to cover your mortgage. The rent (short-term theta collection) pays for your cost of ownership (long-term theta payment). As long as the tenant stays (stock stays near the strike), you’re in good shape.
Example Trade:
Buy-to-open 1 AMZN Mar 21 $230 Call for $9.50
Sell-to-open 1 AMZN Feb 21 $230 Call for $5.00
Net debit: $4.50 (no more than $5.00)

Put Calendars

+P, -P (diff. expirations) Bullish Theta Best when: Near-term options priced higher than far-term Approval: Level 3

What it is: Buy a longer-dated put, sell a shorter-dated put at the same strike.

How it works: Same idea as a call calendar, but with puts. The near-term put you sold loses value faster than the far-term put you bought, and you keep the difference. This works well when you think a stock might drop over time but want to collect some income while you wait.

Picture a farmer who owns land (the longer-dated put) and rents it out for one growing season at a time (the shorter-dated put). Each season’s rent helps pay for the land, and you still own it when the lease is up.
Example Trade:
Buy-to-open 1 SPY Mar 21 $580 Put for $11.00
Sell-to-open 1 SPY Feb 21 $580 Put for $6.00
Net debit: $5.00 (no more than $5.50)

Call Diagonals

+C, -C (diff. exp. & strikes) Bullish Theta Best when: Near-term options priced higher than far-term Approval: Level 3

What it is: Buy a longer-dated ITM call, sell a shorter-dated OTM call.

How it works: This mixes two ideas: different expirations (like a calendar) and different strikes (like a spread). You own a solid ITM call that loses value slowly, and you sell faster-decaying OTM calls against it to collect income over time.

You own a rental property (the long-dated ITM call) and you’re running it as an Airbnb (selling short-term OTM calls over and over). The property has lasting value, and you bring in income from short-term guests.
Example Trade:
Buy-to-open 1 MSFT Apr 17 $400 Call for $28.00
Sell-to-open 1 MSFT Feb 21 $430 Call for $8.00
Net debit: $20.00 (no more than $21.00)

Put Diagonals

+P, -P (diff. exp. & strikes) Bullish Theta Best when: Near-term options priced higher than far-term Approval: Level 3

What it is: Buy a longer-dated OTM put, sell a shorter-dated ITM put.

How it works: You own a longer-dated put as your base position and sell a shorter-dated, closer-to-the-money put to collect income. As the short put loses value over time, you keep that money. If the stock drops, your longer-dated put gains value too.

Think of it like owning a piece of land (the long-dated put) and leasing it out season by season (the short-dated put). You earn rent while holding the land for its long-term value.
Example Trade:
Buy-to-open 1 QQQ Apr 17 $480 Put for $15.00
Sell-to-open 1 QQQ Feb 21 $500 Put for $8.50
Net debit: $6.50 (no more than $7.00)

Long Strangles

+C, +P (buy a call and a put) Bearish Theta Best when: Expecting a big move in either direction Approval: Level 2

What it is: Buy an OTM call AND buy an OTM put at the same expiration.

How it works: You win if the stock moves a lot in either direction. You lose if it sits still, because both options lose value over time.

You’re betting on a storm without knowing if it’ll be a hurricane or a blizzard. You’ve bought both an umbrella and a snow shovel. If the weather stays mild, you wasted money on both. If something big happens either way, one of them pays off big.
Example Trade:
Buy-to-open 1 NFLX Feb 21 $950 Call for $22.00
Buy-to-open 1 NFLX Feb 21 $850 Put for $18.00
Total debit: $40.00 (no more than $42.00)

Zero Theta: The Balanced Scale

When You BUY Options

Theta works against you. Every day, your option loses value just from time passing. It’s like paying rent to stay in the trade.

When You SELL Options

Theta works for you. Every day, you collect value as the option decays. It’s like being the landlord collecting rent.

The Capitol Gains Edge

By combining buys and sells that cancel each other’s theta, you can hold as long as you need without time decay eating into your position. You’re living rent-free while waiting for your trade idea to play out.

Trade Payoff Diagrams

Long Call  +C

You make money when the stock rises above your breakeven (strike + premium paid).

You lose money when the stock stays flat or drops. The most you can lose is the premium you paid. There’s no limit to how much you can make. The higher the stock goes, the more you profit.

Long Put  +P

You make money when the stock falls below your breakeven (strike − premium paid).

You lose money when the stock stays flat or rises. The most you can lose is the premium you paid. The further the stock drops, the more you profit.

Bull Call Spread  +C, -C

You make money when the stock rises above the breakeven. Max profit hits when the stock reaches the short strike or higher.

You lose money when the stock stays below the breakeven. The most you can lose is the debit (the amount you paid to enter). Your profit is capped at the difference between the two strikes minus the debit.

Bear Put Spread  +P, -P

You make money when the stock falls below the breakeven. Max profit hits when the stock reaches the short strike or lower.

You lose money when the stock stays above the breakeven. The most you can lose is the debit. Your profit is capped at the difference between the two strikes minus the debit.

Long Strangle  +C, +P

You make money when the stock makes a big move in either direction, past either breakeven point.

You lose money when the stock stays between the two strikes. The most you can lose is the total premium paid for both options. There’s no limit to how much you can make if the stock moves far enough.

Putting It All Together

Our approach is about matching the right structure to the right environment:

Stock actually moving + cheap options → Buy outright calls or puts, let movement do the work

Expensive options + stock not moving → Use zero-theta spreads, don’t pay inflated rent

Expecting stock to land at a specific price → Calendars and diagonals, collect theta while you wait

Uncertain direction but certain something big is coming → Strangles, pay for the optionality

The Bottom Line

The zero-theta design means you’re not fighting time decay while waiting for a trade idea to play out. You’re set up to wait as long as you need, which is a big advantage when you feel strongly about where a stock is headed but can’t predict exactly when it’ll get there.
