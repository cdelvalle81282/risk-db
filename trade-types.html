The Core Philosophy

Every Capitol Gains trade is designed with two legs that balance each other:

Theta 1

The high-conviction side, where the team expects the stock to move in a specific direction.

Theta 2

The upside potential side, which can produce bonus gains if the stock really runs.

The goal is to combine these two sides so that theta equals zero.

What is Theta?

Theta is the daily cost of holding an option — think of it like rent you pay every day just to stay in the trade.

When You BUY an Option

Theta works against you. Every day that passes, your option loses a little value even if the stock doesn’t move. This is called “time decay.”

When You SELL an Option

Theta works for you. You collect that daily rent from whoever bought the option.

Zero Theta = Living Rent-Free

When the rent you’re paying on one side equals the rent you’re collecting on the other, you can hold the position as long as you need without time decay eating into your profits. You’re waiting for your trade thesis to play out — on the house.

Strike Selection — Where to Place Your Bets

Options come in three flavors based on where their “strike price” sits relative to the current stock price:

At-the-Money (ATM)

The strike price is right at or very close to where the stock is trading now.

Stock is at $100, you buy the $100 call.

ATM options have the highest raw dollar theta because they’re sitting at maximum uncertainty.

In-the-Money (ITM)

The option already has real value built in.

Stock is at $100, you buy the $90 call. That call is worth at least $10 right now.

ITM options have the smallest theta relative to what you paid. They decay slower as a percentage.

Out-of-the-Money (OTM)

The option has no real value yet — purely a bet on future movement.

Stock is at $100, you buy the $110 call. Worthless unless stock climbs above $110.

OTM options have massive theta relative to their price. They decay fast percentage-wise.

The Decay Hierarchy

For Calls: ITM > ATM > OTM   (ITM decays slowest, OTM decays fastest as %)
For Puts: OTM > ATM > ITM   (reversed because put values work opposite)

Expiration Selection — How Much Time to Buy

Options have expiration dates — the day they either become valuable or expire worthless. How far out you go matters:

Near-Term Expirations

Days to a few weeks out. Have the most theta. Time decay accelerates dramatically as expiration approaches — like a candle burning faster as it gets shorter.

Far-Term Expirations

Months out. Have the least theta. The decay is gentler because there’s still plenty of time for things to happen.

Think of it like buying milk. Milk expiring tomorrow is practically worthless at the store. Milk expiring in two weeks still has most of its value. Options work the same way.

Understanding Volatility — The Two Types

Volatility is how much a stock moves around. Capitol Gains tracks two kinds:

Implied Volatility (IV)

What the options market expects to happen. High IV means options are expensive because traders are pricing in big moves. Low IV means options are cheap.

Think of IV as the weather forecast. It’s what people think will happen.
Realized Volatility (RV)

What’s actually happening. How much is the stock really moving day to day?

Think of RV as the actual weather. It’s what’s happening outside your window right now.

The relationship between these two drives trade selection.

The Decision Matrix

The Key Insight

When a stock is actually moving (high RV), own options to capture that movement. When options are expensive but the stock is quiet (high IV, low RV), use balanced spreads so you’re not paying inflated daily rent while waiting.

The Trade Types Explained

Long Calls (Bull Calls)

+C Best when: High RV, Low IV

What it is: Simply buying a call option — a bet the stock will go up.

You’re buying a lottery ticket, but one where the drawing happens every day and the stock’s movement determines if you win. You want cheap tickets (low IV) on a game that’s actually paying out frequently (high RV).
Example Trade:
Buy-to-open 1 MSFT Jan 26 $420 Call for $8.50 (no more than $9.00)

Long Puts (Bear Puts)

+P Best when: High RV, Low IV

What it is: Buying a put option — a bet the stock will go down.

Same as above, just betting on the other direction.
Example Trade:
Buy-to-open 1 TSLA Feb 21 $350 Put for $12.00 (no more than $12.50)

Long Call Spreads (Bull Call Spreads)

+C, -C Zero Theta Best when: Higher IV

What it is: Buy an ITM call, sell an OTM call at a higher strike. Both same expiration.

How it works: You’re buying the right to profit if the stock goes up, but you’re capping your upside by selling a call above. The short strike is your destination — that’s where maximum profit occurs.

You’re buying a ticket to a concert (the long call), but you’re selling the backstage pass rights (the short call) to offset the cost. You get to enjoy the show up to a point, but you’ve given up the chance for the ultimate VIP experience.
Stock at $100. Buy the $95 call, sell the $105 call. You profit as the stock rises toward $105, with max profit at $105 or above. Your cost is reduced because you sold that $105 call.
Example Trade:
Buy-to-open 1 AAPL Mar 21 $220 Call for $12.00
Sell-to-open 1 AAPL Mar 21 $235 Call for $5.50
Net debit: $6.50 (no more than $7.00)

Short Call Spreads (Bear Call Spreads)

-C, +C Bullish Theta Best when: Higher IV, stock flat or down

What it is: Sell an ITM call, buy an OTM call at a higher strike. The reverse of above.

How it works: You’re collecting premium by selling a call and buying a cheaper one above as protection. The short strike is your destination — you want the stock at or below that level at expiration.

You’re the casino now. You’re betting the stock won’t go up much, collecting premium from someone who thinks it will. The long call is your insurance policy capping your losses if you’re wrong.
Example Trade:
Sell-to-open 1 NVDA Feb 21 $140 Call for $8.00
Buy-to-open 1 NVDA Feb 21 $150 Call for $4.00
Net credit: $4.00 (no less than $3.75)

Long Put Spreads (Bear Put Spreads)

+P, -P Bearish Theta Best when: Higher IV, stock falling

What it is: Buy an ITM put, sell an OTM put at a lower strike.

How it works: You’re betting on a decline but capping your profit at the short strike. The short strike is your destination.

You’re buying home insurance (the long put) but agreeing that you’ll only be covered down to a certain damage level (the short put). Cheaper than full coverage, but limited protection.
Example Trade:
Buy-to-open 1 META Mar 21 $580 Put for $18.00
Sell-to-open 1 META Mar 21 $560 Put for $11.00
Net debit: $7.00 (no more than $7.50)

Short Put Spreads (Bull Put Spreads)

-P, +P Bullish Theta Best when: Higher IV, stock flat or rising

What it is: Sell an ITM put, buy an OTM put at a lower strike.

How it works: You’re collecting premium betting the stock won’t fall much. The long put below is your safety net.

Example Trade:
Sell-to-open 1 GOOGL Feb 21 $190 Put for $6.50
Buy-to-open 1 GOOGL Feb 21 $180 Put for $3.00
Net credit: $3.50 (no less than $3.25)

Cash-Secured Puts

-P Bullish Theta Best when: High probability, Higher IV

What it is: Selling an OTM put without any offsetting position — you’re naked but you have the cash to buy shares if assigned.

How it works: You sell a put below the current stock price, collecting premium. If the stock stays above your strike, you keep the premium. If it falls below, you’re obligated to buy shares at that strike — but you wanted to own them anyway at that price.

You’re getting paid to put in a limit order. Someone pays you to agree that you’ll buy their stock if it drops to a certain price. If it never gets there, you just pocket the payment.
Example Trade:
Sell-to-open 1 AMD Jan 17 $120 Put for $3.20 (no less than $3.00)
(Secure with $12,000 cash in account)

Call Calendars

+C, -C (diff. expirations) Bullish Theta Best when: Higher Theta, Horizontal Vol Skew

What it is: Buy a longer-dated call, sell a shorter-dated call at the same strike.

How it works: The near-term option you sold decays faster than the far-term option you bought. You’re profiting from the difference in decay rates. The strike is your destination — you want the stock to land right there at the short option’s expiration.

You’re renting out a room in your house to cover your mortgage. The rent (short-term theta collection) pays for your cost of ownership (long-term theta payment). As long as the tenant stays (stock stays near the strike), you’re in good shape.
Example Trade:
Buy-to-open 1 AMZN Mar 21 $230 Call for $9.50
Sell-to-open 1 AMZN Feb 21 $230 Call for $5.00
Net debit: $4.50 (no more than $5.00)

Put Calendars

+P, -P (diff. expirations) Bullish Theta Best when: Higher Theta, Horizontal Vol Skew

What it is: Buy a longer-dated put, sell a shorter-dated put at the same strike.

How it works: Same concept as call calendars, just using puts. Often used when you have a bearish lean but want to structure it efficiently.

Example Trade:
Buy-to-open 1 SPY Mar 21 $580 Put for $11.00
Sell-to-open 1 SPY Feb 21 $580 Put for $6.00
Net debit: $5.00 (no more than $5.50)

Call Diagonals

+C, -C (diff. exp. & strikes) Bullish Theta Best when: Higher Theta, Horizontal Vol Skew

What it is: Buy a longer-dated ITM call, sell a shorter-dated OTM call.

How it works: Combines the calendar concept (different expirations) with a spread concept (different strikes). You own a solid ITM call that decays slowly, and you’re selling faster-decaying OTM calls against it over time.

You own a rental property (the long-dated ITM call) and you’re running it as an Airbnb (selling short-term OTM calls repeatedly). The property has lasting value, and you generate income from short-term rentals.
Example Trade:
Buy-to-open 1 MSFT Apr 17 $400 Call for $28.00
Sell-to-open 1 MSFT Feb 21 $430 Call for $8.00
Net debit: $20.00 (no more than $21.00)

Put Diagonals

+P, -P (diff. exp. & strikes) Bullish Theta Best when: Higher Theta, Horizontal Vol Skew

What it is: Buy a longer-dated OTM put, sell a shorter-dated ITM put.

How it works: Same diagonal concept applied to puts.

Example Trade:
Buy-to-open 1 QQQ Apr 17 $480 Put for $15.00
Sell-to-open 1 QQQ Feb 21 $500 Put for $8.50
Net debit: $6.50 (no more than $7.00)

Long Strangles

+C, +P Bearish Theta Best when: Higher Realized Volatility expected

What it is: Buy an OTM call AND buy an OTM put at the same expiration.

How it works: You win if the stock moves significantly in either direction. You lose if it sits still because both options decay.

You’re betting on a storm without knowing if it’ll be a hurricane or a blizzard. You’ve bought both an umbrella and a snow shovel. If the weather stays mild, you wasted money on both. If something big happens either way, one of them pays off big.
Example Trade:
Buy-to-open 1 NFLX Feb 21 $950 Call for $22.00
Buy-to-open 1 NFLX Feb 21 $850 Put for $18.00
Total debit: $40.00 (no more than $42.00)

Zero Theta: The Balanced Scale

When You BUY Options

Theta works against you. Every day, your option loses value just from time passing. It’s like paying rent to stay in the trade.

When You SELL Options

Theta works for you. Every day, you collect value as the option decays. It’s like being the landlord collecting rent.

The Capitol Gains Edge

By combining buys and sells that cancel each other’s theta, you can hold as long as you need without time decay eating into your position. You’re living rent-free while waiting for your thesis to play out.

Bet on big move in either direction. Profits from large moves up or down, loses if stock stays flat.

Putting It All Together

The Capitol Gains approach is about matching the right structure to the right environment:

Stock actually moving + cheap options → Buy outright calls or puts, let movement do the work

Expensive options + stock not moving → Use zero-theta spreads, don’t pay inflated rent

Expecting stock to land at a specific price → Calendars and diagonals, collect theta while you wait

Uncertain direction but certain something big is coming → Strangles, pay for the optionality

The Bottom Line

The zero-theta design means the team isn’t fighting time decay while waiting for their thesis to play out. They’re structured to wait as long as needed — which is a significant edge when you have high conviction about where a stock is headed but can’t predict exactly when it’ll get there.
